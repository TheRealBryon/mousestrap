Introduction

Disney Death Tour provides an emplaced critique at the site of Disney World’s Magic Kingdom in order to memorialize those who have lost their lives at the park. By constructing digital, and therefore unremovable, monuments at the locations of the many deaths that Disney has sought to cover up, this project challenges the dominant narratives of the park while reflecting on the abject sacrifices endemic to the tourist industry.

An Undertaking in Critical Tourism

“[T]he process of MEmorializing begins with a sting (punctum) received from a news item, a story from the daily dose of information circulated by journalism.”—Gregory L. Ulmer, Electronic Monuments

“A Walt Disney World custodian plunged 40 feet to his death Sunday morning after clinging to the outside of a rising cable car that had swept him from a platform.”—Orlando Sentinel, 14 February, 1999

Our Disney Death Tour starts with a single tragedy:

Raymond Barlow, 65, was on a platform for the Skyway in Fantasyland when the cable-car ride was switched on for the park's opening, sending one of the gondolas toward the part-time worker. The startled Clermont resident grabbed the four-person gondola and struggled to pull himself inside, park employees told investigators. On the way up, he apparently was looking for a place to land and let go over a flower bed, snapping some tree branches on the way down, the workers said.
(“Worker At Disney Plunges To His Death”)

The Skyway from which he fell was torn down six months later. Although Disney was fined $4,500 for “a ‘serious’ violation of safety standards,” The Orlando Sentinel reported that, “The ride's closing is not a result of any concerns about its safety.” Bob Sehlinger, author of The Unofficial Guide to Walt Disney World, commented, “The five-minute Skyway ride, though pleasant, was probably costly to maintain, and Disney figured it could make better use of the space.”

The incident demonstrates a few principles regarding narrative and space. Whatever the official story may be, the close proximity between Barlow’s death and shuttering the Skyway certainly seems suspicious. Or at least it wouldn’t come as a surprise if the actual story differed from the official account. More importantly, the two phenomena actually happened; Raymond Barlow died and the Skyway closed down. Despite all efforts to control a corporate image, sinister or practical, no imagineer can stop people from dying at Disney. As for the ride, its removal further highlights erasure and rewriting space. Sehlinger’s comments reflect practical business interests, but Barlow’s death adds a macabre poignancy to the high costs of maintaining a theme park.

Around 30 people have died at Disney theme parks since 1977 (an exact figure is difficult to cobble together). Most were the result of preexisting conditions or natural causes, what one report calls “not surprising.” Some were the result of freak accidents. In many cases, families pursued legal action and reached monetary settlements with the Disney corporation. For reasons explored later in this text, however, Raymond Barlow’s death stands out. The tour necessarily starts with him.

Criticizing the Mouse

“It’s company policy… If guests have the nerve to die, they wait, like unwanted calories, until they’ve crossed the line and can do so safely off property. [...] Thus, no one gets sick or hurt at Disney; no one dies at Disney, no one leaves Disney unhappy or is fired for no reason; and no one writes bad things about the park once they’re gone.”—Inside the Mouse

That “no one dies at Disney” paints a dystopian picture of corporate control, mediation of park space so authoritarian as to censor death itself. A cover-up. A conspiracy. Disney scrutinizes what enters and leaves its property such that truth only circulates in the off-record whispers of its employees:

We had a guy last summer who went to EPCOT, stood under the golf ball, took a gun, and blew his head off. But he didn’t die. He stood right there in front of all those tourists and went “cluck” and brains blew everywhere. But he didn’t die there. The medic told me that they are not allowed to let them die there. Keep them alive by artificial means until they’re off Disney property, like there’s an imaginary line in the road and they go, “He’s alive, he’s alive, he’s dead.” (Qtd. in Inside the Mouse 115)

The absurdity of it all–denial of a bullet in the brain’s finality, no further explanation of “artificial means,” and of course the arbitrary border between life and death–contributes to and almost certainly stems from Disney’s perceived omnipotence.

DDT is premised on an urban legend, the first challenge to our credibility. Online fact-checker Snopes.com takes on the claim, “Disney can legitmately [sic] claim that no one has ever died at one of their theme parks, because they always ensure that accident victims are removed from park property before being declared dead” (“Declared Deaths at Disney”). Though it renders a “false” verdict, Snopes acknowledges “This legend is a tricky subject to tackle, because it’s based upon the fine (and often confusing) distinction between actual death and declared death.” To clarify our assumptions, at no point do we take for granted the Disney death myth as law of the land. DDT instead explores how Disney deaths circulate within a network of lore, industry, place, and community discourse.

Critical approaches to Disney theme parks are nothing new. Thibaut Clément’s excellent review of literature traces the tension between intellectual work and mass entertainment exemplified by Disney. Efforts to identify the “locus of control” in Disney’s parks traversed the high-brow/low-brow debates of the last century. Early intellectual suspicion emerges as early as 1958. Other voices saw in Disneyland a restoration of the public sphere, of performance and participation. Recent work emphasizes the productive rather than pessimistic possibilities in commercial tourist spaces:

In assigning various degrees of agency to the parks’ participants, critics have gradually displaced the locus of control for the parks’ meaning, design, and operations from the Disney corporation to the individual user and the company’s socio-economic context, paying increasing attention to the audience’s interpretive activity and the parks’ competitive environment. (8)

Thibaut concludes “Visitors are not ‘cultural dopes’ but rather actively reconstruct available meanings to elaborate strategies and pursue motives of their own” (8). Seeking the locus of control shifts from identifying the presence of agency to the more nuanced assessment of “how much credit the parks’ various participants must be given in shaping and assigning meaning to the parks’ environment” (9).

In 1995, the cultural studies ensemble The Project on Disney published Inside the Mouse: Work and Play at Disney World, which conducts a critical examination of and in Disney theme parks. In part the book is a model. It weighs the self-conscious guilt experienced by critics turned tourists (“The Problem with Pleasure”), suggests playfully subversive negotiations of park space (“The Alternative Ride”), and can’t resist a degree of anti-authority snark (“Working at the Rat”). It also helps circulate the “no one dies at Disney” legend as a counter-narrative which escapes corporate oversight. For our purposes, the Project on Disney serves as a precedent, a source for the myth, and ultimately a point of departure.

By including an image of settled scholarship, we hope to clarify our intentions for DDT—what it is, and what it is not. It is not an exposé revealing corporate secrets, nor a critically aloof reading of Disney parks. Neither do we wish to state the obvious: of course visitors cultivate personal meanings in even the most mediated of spaces. Whereas previous critical work formulates Disney parks in terms of either the passive reception of signs or the active construction of individual interpretations, of dominant ideologies or subversive subjectivities, DDT introduces new writings into the space itself. At the same time, we explore theories of writing and of writing in relation to space. DDT employs as much its methods–MEmorality, psychogeography, augmented reality, critical tourism–as an inquiry into the limits of our methods.
