Emplaced Composition

In this section we develop an understanding of critical tourism, which we define as embodied participation in public writing spaces through travel. The prototype to critical tourism, theoria, names the ancient Greek practice of investigation through travel. According to Gregory L. Ulmer, the first theorists were also the first tourists (5). Theoria “implied a complex but organic mode of active observation—a perceptual system that included asking questions, listening to stories and local myths, and feeling as well as hearing and seeing” (E. V. Walter, qtd. in Electronic Monuments). Early theorists sought out facts obscured by rumor and myth. Disney Death Tour inverts theoria’s original mission by exploring not just the creative, but the critical potential of myth. Just as theoria depends on physical travel and perceptual attunement, emplaced composition emphasizes “the role specific places play in rhetorical invention” (1).

To critical tourism we introduce “Emplaced Composition” (Jones). Emplaced composition responds to environmental catastrophe rendered moot by abstraction by emphasizing the material specifics of place. For an emplaced ethical model, Jones focuses on Silver Springs, FL, interestingly one of the state’s main tourist attractions prior to Disney World. Today visitors can still experience the Springs’ iconic glass-bottom boat tours, which Wendy Adams King identifies as a “technology for viewing the crystal springs from the safety and comfort of a boat tour” (qtd. in Jones 12). However, as nitrate runoff and salinization cloud the crystal waters, the glass-bottom screen metaphorically distances viewers from the material particulars of Silver Springs and promotes a “bifurcated world: separating nature and culture, mind and body, subject and environment, word and world, human and other” (4). Counter to glass-bottom mediation, Jones privileges William Bartram, who famously wrote of Silver Springs’ crystal waters from the emplaced vantage point of his canoe.

Disney frustrates emplaced composition’s core environmental concerns. As caricatures, its castles, kingdoms, and countries represent the ultimate bifurcation of nature and culture. Our tour becomes an emplaced composition by introducing specific loss to generalized place. Morbid and grotesque, our attunement to death and the material loss of life at Disney signals nature-culture’s indivisibility. No-one-dies-at-Disney operates by ceding the locus of control to total corporate mediation. The myth’s absurdity signals rupture of the modernist project.

The Heroism of the Tourist

“Practices associated with death and remembrance are highly ritualized in almost all societies where there is a belief system based upon the separation of body and soul and an afterlife or reincarnation, whether or not a ‘final resting place’ for the corporeal remains is designated.”—Dark Tourism

[This section is mostly junk and needs major revision.] Modern commercial tourism’s analog to Bartram’s canoe is David Foster Wallace’s luxury cruiseship. Wallace’s extended travelogue “A Supposedly Fun Thing I’ll Never Do Again” gives us an image of the self-conscious intellectual turned neurotic tourist. Aboard the Zenith (which reminiscent of “Working at the Rat” he redubs Nadir), Wallace thumbs his nose at bovine commercial tourism. In a tone frequently used in academic responses to Disney, his anxiety indicates a loss of control: “I pay for the privilege of handing over to trained professionals responsibility not just for my experience but for me interpretation of that experience -- i.e. my pleasure” (268). At the same time, Wallace attributes our willingness to surrender agency to a deeper anxiety:

Here’s the thing. A vacation is a respite from unpleasantness, and since consciousness of death and decay are unpleasant, it may seem weird the Americans’ ultimate fantasy vacation involves being plunked down in an enormous primordial engine of death and decay. But on the 7NC Luxury Cruise, we are skillfully enabled in the construction of various fantasies of triumph over just this death and decay. (264)

While not bitterly dismissive, Wallace forecloses tourism’s critically productive capacity. Ironically, his descriptions of bovine tourists and hyper-mediation actually contribute to a canonical version of tourism. Emplaced composition not only disrupts such strict codification, it encourages invention of alter-narratives from the particulars of embodied experience. However, “A Supposedly Fun Thing I’ll Never Do Again” does strike at the intermingling of death, tourism, and fantasy this project explores.

From the Greek root for personified death, thanatourism includes visits to battlefields, concentration camps, famous gravesites, etc.—any attraction to the spectacle of death. In Dark Tourism: The Attraction of Death and Disaster, the authors associate tourism with the modernist project’s “ideas of universalism, classification and the liberal democratic state” (7). The era of thanatourism begins with two closely occurring catastrophes: the First World War, an unprecedented industrial clash of modern liberal democracies, and the sinking of the Titanic. The era of thanatourism is also the era of mass media and culture. In a society of the spectacle, death becomes subject to commodification. Dark Tourism posits death’s attraction as a consumptive means of validating individual experience of mass media images.

"Dark," or thanatourism, indicates the postmodern condition. Dark Tourism argues that for a tourist destination to be dark, “global communication technologies play a major part in creating the initial interest” and “the objects of dark tourism themselves appear to introduce anxiety and doubt about the project of modernity” (11). Disney World is not a battlefield or a concentration camp. In its commitment to being the happiest place on earth, we can find the sort of “fantasies of triumph” over death and decay Wallace refers to. DDT “darkens” the Magic Kingdom, countering fantasies of escape with the recognition of structural death.

Encountering death in spaces dedicated to fun seems grotesque, but our purpose is not necessarily to introduce anxiety and doubt for its own sake. MEmorial is rather a means of recognizing and reflecting upon community values enshrined in our collective sacrifices. Connoting a more peaceful intermingling of death and travel (Wikipedia), “thanatourism” more appropriately describes our efforts to avoid immediate ethical judgement; DDT is only a memento mori. Though we distance our project from the use of “Dark Tourism,” the book’s authors identify the central role of the individual in electrate thanatourism: “Pilgrimage has a religious or, at least, mystical significance [...] which contains elements of both a personal physical as well as often a psychological journey for participants” (4). In the next section, we explore embodied individuals as critical actants in networked writing spaces.
